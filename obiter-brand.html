obiter

Brand guide, v0.1. A markdown notes app for people who want their notes to stay their own — plain files, in a folder they picked, readable in fifty years without us.

[…]Obiter dicta — "things said by the way." A judge's remarks that aren't binding, and are often the most interesting part of the ruling.

01 / premise

What we're actually selling

Not features. Not a second brain. The promise is subtraction: no database, no proprietary format, no account, no sync you didn't ask for. Your notes are markdown files on your disk. Obiter is a good way to write them, and the least consequential thing in the stack — if we vanish tomorrow, you lose an editor, not your work.

Everything downstream — the mark, the type, the accent, the copy — is trying to say this thing is honest and it will not surprise you. That's the whole brief. If a design decision doesn't support it, it's decoration.

Three tests

  • The folder test. Could a person open their notes with cat? If a feature breaks that, it doesn't ship.
  • The fifty-year test. Will this file open in 2076? Plain text says yes.
  • The aside test. Is this the interesting thing said in passing, or is it filler? Applies to copy, features, and this document.
[…]The mark is […] — the editorial mark for text elided from a quotation. Brackets hold something; the dots are what was said in passing.

02 / the mark

Logo

Drawn on a 32-unit grid. One module governs everything: the stroke is 3, the dots are 3×3, and every gap — bracket to dot, dot to dot, dot to bracket — is 3. Nothing in the mark is eyeballed. Corners are mitered, caps are butt. No rounding, anywhere, ever.

The bracket arms run to x=10 and x=22, terminating exactly over the centres of the outer dots. That alignment is what makes them read as brackets containing something rather than as stray ticks. Hold it at every size.

Clearspace and minimum size

Clearspace is 6 units (two modules) from the ink edge on all sides. Nothing enters it — not the wordmark, not a container edge, not a nav item.

Minimum size for the master drawing is 32px. Below that it does not scale; it gets redrawn. The 16px cut is a separate file: 2px stroke, 2px dots, snapped to whole pixels, one dot instead of three — three dots plus four gaps cannot fit in 16px without going sub-pixel, and a smudge is worse than an honest simplification.

Misuse

03 / color

Neutrals, and the blue pencil

Six warm-leaning neutrals and one accent. The neutrals are warm by about four percent — enough that the interface doesn't feel clinical, far enough from cream that it doesn't read as a stationery brand.

Rules

  • Pencil marks, it doesn't decorate. It appears where the user has acted or where the app is pointing: cursor, selection, focus ring, active item, link. Never a background, never a hero, never a gradient.
  • One Pencil per view. If two things are blue, neither is the answer.
  • Pencil 500 on Paper fails small text. Use Pencil 600 for any text under 18px. 500 is for fills and 1px+ strokes.
  • The mark is never Pencil. Ink on light, Paper on dark. The logo is not an accent.
  • Danger is unclaimed. Because the accent is blue, red is still free and unambiguous. Don't spend it on anything but destruction.

Still open: dark mode. The neutrals invert cleanly, but Pencil 500 on Ink is uncomfortably hot — it likely needs to shift to 200 as the accent and 500 as the fill. Needs a pass before v1.

[…]Both faces are open-licensed and self-hosted. A product that promises you own your data should not phone a third party for a font on every page load.

04 / typography

The interface speaks. The content is plain.

One rule, and it's conceptual rather than aesthetic: sans is the app talking, mono is your text and the machine's. Buttons, labels, settings, prose on the site — Public Sans. Notes, file paths, code, keys, data, the wordmark — IBM Plex Mono.

The wordmark sits on the mono side on purpose. The name should look like something you typed into a note, because that's what the product is. It's also why the display face here is the utility face — there's no third typeface pretending to be the brand's voice.

Scale

  • display 34 / 1.15 / mono 500 — wordmark and page titles only
  • title 26 / 1.25 / sans 500
  • heading 17 / 1.4 / sans 500
  • body 16 / 1.65 / sans 400
  • small 14 / 1.5 / sans 400 — secondary, never primary content
  • micro 11.5 / 1.4 / mono 400 — labels, eyebrows, metadata

Sentence case everywhere. No title case, no all-caps — caps read as shouting and the brand doesn't shout. Measure caps at 62ch; a notes app that can't set a comfortable line length has no credibility.

05 / voice

Plain, specific, unhurried

Write like you're telling one competent person what the thing does. No urgency, no hype, no confiding. The register is a good README, not a landing page.

Principles

  • Show the folder, don't promise privacy. Anyone can claim to respect your data. Say your notes are .md files in ~/Notes and the claim becomes unnecessary.
  • Specific beats plain. Plain beats clever. In that order, always.
  • The app has no feelings. No "Oops!", no "We're sorry", no exclamation marks in system copy. Errors say what happened and what to do.
  • Nothing is running out. No countdowns, no limited-time, no streaks, no badges. A tool that manufactures urgency about your own notes is a tool that has stopped being yours.
  • Never sell ownership as a feature. It's the premise. Features are things you can turn off.

Words we don't use

06 / tokens

The system

Paste these in. Radius is deliberately near-zero — the mark is mitered and the interface should agree. 3px is the ceiling, and it exists only so inputs don't look broken.

/* obiter — design tokens */
:root {
  /* neutrals */
  --paper:      #FAF9F7;  /* app background */
  --chalk:      #EFEEEA;  /* raised surface, code bg */
  --ash:        #D6D4CD;  /* borders, rules */
  --slate:      #9A9891;  /* metadata, placeholder */
  --graphite:   #55534E;  /* secondary text */
  --ink:        #1C1B18;  /* primary text, the mark */

  /* accent — marks, never decorates */
  --pencil-50:  #EAF6FB;
  --pencil-200: #9EDCF0;
  --pencil-500: #0E8FBC;  /* fills, focus ring, cursor */
  --pencil-600: #0A6E93;  /* text under 18px */

  /* danger — destruction only */
  --danger:     #B3261E;

  /* type */
  --sans: "Public Sans", system-ui, sans-serif;
  --mono: "IBM Plex Mono", ui-monospace, monospace;

  /* space — 8px base */
  --u-1: 4px;   --u-2: 8px;   --u-3: 12px;
  --u-4: 16px;  --u-6: 24px;  --u-8: 32px;
  --u-12: 48px; --u-16: 64px;

  /* form */
  --radius:   2px;
  --radius-lg:3px;
  --border:   1px solid var(--ash);
  --focus:    0 0 0 2px var(--pencil-500);
  --measure:  62ch;
  --control:  32px;
}

Applying them

  • Borders over shadows. One hairline in Ash. No elevation, no depth — the app is a text file, not a stack of cards.
  • Focus is always visible. 2px Pencil, 2px offset, on everything. A keyboard-first tool that hides focus is lying about being keyboard-first.
  • Controls are 32px. Dense, but this is a writing tool for people who'd rather see another line of text.
  • No gradients, no blur, no glass. Not a style preference — those are all ways of implying depth the product doesn't have.

Still open: the type ramp assumes a desktop window. Tauri gives us mobile eventually; the 32px control and 11.5px micro won't survive a touch target audit. Fork the scale when we get there rather than compromising the desktop one now.
